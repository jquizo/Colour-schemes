About

Understanding what emotions certain colours elicit will help you choose a colour to go along with your design, brand or website.

It allows you to communicate your vision or plan to people easier if you know what emotions you want to elicit in your customers.

Colour Wheel

The colour wheel consists of three primary colours (red, yellow, blue or RGB for short), three secondary colours (created by combining primary colours (purple, orange, green), and six tertiary colours (created by combining primary and secondary colours.

Tertiary colours are located in between the primary and secondary colours on the colour wheel shown.

Colour Types

Warm colours

On one side of the colour wheel we have the warm colours. Warm colours elicit feelings of warmth and energy, as the colours are associated with fire, the sun and heat.

Cool colours

On the other side of the colour wheel we have the cool colours. Cool colours elicit feelings of coolness and calm, as the colours are associated with water, the sky, ice and grass.

Colour Schemes

Complementary

These colours are on opposite sides of the colour wheel. This combination is a high contrast, vibrant combination and works well when you want something to stand out, though the scheme must be managed properly so it is not tiresome. An example of this is Christmas colours.

Analogous

Analogous colours are adjacent to each other on the colour wheel. When using analogous colours, usually one colour is used to dominate, a second to support and the third will accent. This colour scheme is pleasing to the eye and depending on the colours can be quite calming.

Triadic

A triadic colour scheme uses three colours that are evenly spaced on the colour wheel. This colour scheme is vibrant, but less than complementary colour schemes. While you can make things stand out, it should be balanced, for example using one colour to dominate and two accent colours.
